Questions Someone Might Conceivably Ask At Some Point

Where do Solidarity Economies come from?

There is nothing new about what we are doing. The diverse origins of the Solidarity Economy range from Robert Owen’s 18th century experiments with workers’ cooperatives, to the Landless Workers’ Movements of the Global South, to French theories of Economie Sociale, to the development of Mondragon federation of worker cooperatives in the Basque region of Spain, to the unemployed workers’ movements in Argentina and, more recently, Chicago.

Why a map?

One of our major sources of inspiration was SolidarityNYC. We were influenced by their choice to start with a mapping project. A good map can serve two functions right away: it can be an immediately useful, usable tool that lets people retrieve information, and it can help give an identity to something invisible. It also was a needed step for us to understand what already exists around us. Part of the joy of this project is the constant discovery of places already existing in our neighborhoods and towns.

There are now around a dozen known Solidarity Economy Maps in the US, and internationally we're most excited by Brazil's for its massive 22,000 organization Economia Solidária.

We're also excited by the possibilities around printing a map for places in the community to feature.

If you want to map your own region, our code and support is yours; you should also check out this thorough mapping tutorial from Shareable.net!

Wait, what is a Solidarity Economy again?

In one sense, a Solidarity Economy can be thought of as a way of relating economically that identifies shared values and makes those values central to how it develops and interconnects. Not purely capitalist, socialist, or any-ist, it is a shared step forward that expresses itself through a variety of structures, formal and informal, public and private, old and new.

What are the values behind a Solidarity Economy? Brazil cites seven that we find it easy to agree with:

  1. Self-management
  2. Democratization of economic relations
  3. Co-operation instead of forced competition
  4. Valuing diversity. Human beings are more important than profits
  5. Valuing local knowledge, constant learning and training
  6. Social justice and emancipation
  7. Protection of the environment

Solidarity Economies tend to also express themselves through certain structures - community land trusts, alternative currencies, CSAs, credit unions, social investment funds, participatory budgeting, fair trade, an increase or revitalization of the commons - and many new ones yet emerging to meet the needs of our rapidly-changing world.

How can I help?

The biggest thing of all is to add to the map! We are too few now to collect good data or to know everything that belongs on here. Please add submissions to the map and we'll review them as soon as we can.

It's also really important to use the map and share it with others who are new to the region or would like to learn about how to participate in a more humane economy.

You may notice the map has a lot more density in some towns than others, even though it is designed for the whole Piedmont region. You would make our day by volunteering to add places in Greensboro, Burlington, Raleigh, or any other town considered part of the Piedmont region!
